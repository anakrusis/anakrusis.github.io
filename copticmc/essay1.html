This passage consists of three cleft sentences which correspond closely with Coptic, and an aorist sentence which does not correspond straightforwardly with Coptic, but can probably be reconstructed based on the evidence that exists.

The aorist prefix ḫr

In Demotic the aorist was usually written as ḫr + [i]a verb in the sḏm⸗f form[/i]. A periphrastic form ḫr + ỉr ("do" in sḏm⸗f form) + [i]a verb in the infinitive[/i] also existed and became increasingly common in the Roman period [c]dvs-87[/c]. It is the periphrastic form that survives in Coptic as [k]ϣⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ϣⲁ⸗[/k] [c]ce-251[/c] [c]vy-269a[/c]. The non-periphrastic ḫr + sḏm⸗f form is the one found in this passage, and it does not survive in Coptic, so the pronunciation must be reconstructed. The first uncertainty to look for is the vowel quality of ḫr.

First, the aorist forms in different Coptic dialects should be compared. Sahidic and Bohairic both have [k]ϣⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ϣⲁ⸗[/k], and Fayyumic is similarly [k]ϣⲁⲗⲉ-[/k], [k]ϣⲁ⸗[/k]. Sahidic also has some unusual forms attested [k]ⲉϣⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ⲛϣⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ⲛϣⲁ⸗[/k] (this one in Fayyumic too). I do not know if the initial [k]ⲉ-[/k] and [k]ⲛ-[/k] are epenthetic, or if something else is occurring here.
Akhmimic and related dialects preserve a less reduced prepronominal form: Akhmimic [k]ⳉⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ⳉⲁⲣⲉ⸗[/k], Subakhmimic [k]ϣⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ϣⲁⲣⲉ⸗[/k], and also Akhmimic-influenced Sahidic [k]ⲉϣⲁⲣⲉ⸗[/k] [c]cd-583a[/c].
In all of these, the vowel immediately following [k]ϣ/ⳉ[/k] is consistently [k]ⲁ[/k] in both the pronominal and prepronominal forms, and between all dialects.

But because all of the above Coptic forms are derived from the periphrastic ḫr ỉr⸗f, they may not provide enough information to deduce the vowel quality of ḫr alone. Vowel reduction is very likely. This is seen in similar situations with auxiliary verbs; for example Allen reconstructs ỉr⸗f sḏm /i.ri.af.'sa.ḏam/ > [k]ⲁϥⲥⲱⲧⲙ[/k] [c]aep-136[/c].
So it may be useful to compare other examples where a particle is attached to ỉr⸗f; especially those where the non-attached form survives as well, to see how the vowel quality is affected by the neighboring ỉr⸗f, and by possible vowel reduction:

• The optative[d]𓅓𓇋𓇋𓏲[/d]my [i]verb in sḏm⸗f[/i] ( > [k]ⲙⲁ-[/k] ) and its periphrastic form[d]𓅓𓇋𓇋𓏲𓁹:𓂋*𓏭𓇋𓏲[/d]my ỉre [i]verb in infinitive[/i] ( > [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉ⸗[/k] ) both survive in Coptic [c]ce-77, 88[/c] [c]dvs-139-142[/c]. All dialects have the same vowels in [k]ⲙⲁ-[/k] [c]cd-155b[/c] and [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉ-[/k] (Fayyumic [k]ⲙⲁⲗⲉ-[/k]) [c]cd-182b[/c].
In addition to the unstressed ỉr⸗ verb in [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉ-[/k], [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉ⸗[/k], Coptic also preserves this same verb in a stressed position in [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲟⲛ[/k] "let us (go)" ( < my ỉr⸗n ) [c]cd-182b[/c] [c]lambdin-135[/c]. The vowel quality [k]ⲁ[/k] in the first syllable does not change here whether ỉr⸗n is stressed (as in [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲟⲛ[/k]) or unstressed (as in [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉⲛ- / ⲙⲁⲣⲛ̄-[/k]).
Additionally, my survives in its stressed form as Bohairic [k]ⲙⲟⲓ[/k]; nonstandard Fayyumic [k]ⲙⲁⲓ [/k] [c]cd-159a[/c] [c]ep-276[/c] [c]vy-103[/c].

• The temporal is another construction which had a particle n-ḏr.t + [i]verb in sḏm⸗f[/i], and whose periphrastic form n-ḏr.t ỉr⸗f + [i]verb in infinitive[/i] is all that survives in Coptic [k]ⲛⲧⲉⲣⲉ-, ⲛⲧⲉⲣⲉ⸗[/k] [c]dvs-147-149[/c] [c]two-81[/c]. The particle had many spelling variations; in ꜥOnchsheshonqy [d]𓈖2:𓏏:𓄿𓏭[/d]n-tꜣỉ, and in other texts [d]𓅓1𓏲:𓏏[/d]mtw, the same as the conjunctive ( > Coptic [k]ⲛⲧⲉ-[/k] [c]ce-111[/c] ). These spellings suggest, as Černy gives, that the particle by itself was pronounced [k]ⲛⲧⲉ-[/k] as well [c]ce-112[/c].
But this temporal form is not used in all Coptic dialects, and it is not consistently vocalized among the dialects in which it is attested: Sahidic [k]ⲛⲧⲉⲣⲉ-[/k], Akhmimic [k](ⲛ)ⲧⲁⲣⲉ-[/k] [c]cd-232a[/c]. Bohairic uses a different construction for this, the focalized or second perfect [k]ⲉⲧⲁ-[/k].

• The negative aorist was periphrastic bw ỉr⸗f [i]verb in infinitive[/i] in Demotic [c]dvs-96-99[/c]. An exception to this: a non-periphrastic negative aorist verb bw rḫ⸗f "not know" survives in Coptic as [k]ⲙⲉϣⲉ- ⲙⲉϣⲁ⸗[/k] [c]ce-96[/c] [c]vy-119[/c].
The Coptic descendants of the negative aorist vary quite a bit in regard to the first vowel. [k]ⲙⲉⲣⲉ-, ⲙⲉ⸗ [/k] are the usual Sahidic forms (and attested somewhere in Fayyumic as well); Akhmimic (and somewhere in Sahidic) has [k]ⲙⲁⲣⲉ-, ⲙⲁ⸗[/k]; Fayyumic has [k]ⲙⲉⲗⲉ-[/k]; Bohairic [k]ⲙⲡⲁⲣⲉ-, ⲙⲡⲁ⸗[/k]. [c]cd-182b[/c] [c]ce-88[/c] [c]vy-119[/c]
The descendants of bw rḫ⸗f are found in Sahidic [k]ⲙⲉϣⲉ-, ⲙⲉϣⲁ⸗[/k] and Akhmimic [k]ⲙⲉⳉⲉ⸗[/k] (once) [c]cd-201b[/c] [c]vy-128[/c]. Vycichl divides the word as [k]ⲙ-[/k] (negation) and [k]ⲉϣⲁ⸗[/k] (the verb), so the vowel in bw is probably completely reduced.

The sḏm⸗f after ḫr

The prospective sḏm⸗f

In a causative sentence such as tỉ ... ḫpr [i]noun[/i] "cause that [i]noun[/i] comes into being, i.e. acquire [i]noun[/i]", the second verb is a prospective sḏm⸗f [c]two-84[/c]. The vocalization of this construction is preserved in Coptic in the form of the large T-causative family of infinitives. [c]lambdin-112-114[/c]
