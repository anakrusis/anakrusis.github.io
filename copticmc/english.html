ⲛⲱⲟⲩⲧⲥ — Notes

1.) Coptic plosives do not have a voiced-unvoiced distinction. Bohairic does distinguish between aspirated and unaspirated stops [k]ⲫ/ⲡ, ⲑ/ⲧ,[/k] and [k]ⲭ/ⲕ[/k]. In the Coptic script for English, the aspiration of the consonants is written, and the voicing is usually not written. For example, /b/ and unaspirated /p/ are both written [k]ⲡ[/k], while aspirated /pʰ/ is [k]ⲫ[/k].
2.) Note that [k]ⲃ[/k] is not used for /b/, because it is not a stop consonant at all; it has become a fricative /β/ in Coptic, and is closer in sound to the English /v/. Arabic loanwords in Coptic use [k]ⲡ[/k] for ب /b/, while [k]ⲃ[/k] is instead used for ف /f/ (Richter BiCtOS 517-518).
3.) In Bohairic [k]ϫ[/k] and [k]ϭ[/k] are an aspirated and unaspirated pair /tʃ/ and /tʃʰ/. In Sahidic they are distinguished by quality: /tʃ/ and /kʲ/ respectively. Coptic script for English is more similar to Bohairic, in the same way as the other plosives [1]: [k]ϫ[/k] /dʒ/ (perhaps also unstressed /tʃ/), and [k]ϭ[/k] /tʃ/. For example, "church" could be written [k]ϭⲣ̄ϫ[/k] (looks better to me) or [k]ϭⲣ̄ϭ[/k].
4.) Coptic has no dental fricatives. But it is well attested that, when borrowing words from languages which have dental fricatives, they are replaced by stops (Peust EP 90) such as [k]ⲑ, ⲧ, ⲇ[/k] variously (van Putten C-A 92).
The choice of [k]ⲇ[/k] for /ð/ is not based on any dialect of Coptic, but rather because the letter is otherwise barely used, and it matches the Modern Greek pronunciation. Arabic loanwords into Coptic with ذ‎ are most often written [k]ⲧ[/k] (Richter BiCtOS 518).
The choice of [k]ⲑ[/k] for /θ/ is based on what is most commonly attested (Peust EP 90), but I suppose that [k]ⲧ[/k] or [k]ⲇ[/k] could also be used here. It is more important that /ð/ has a distinct letter as it is the much more common phoneme (appearing in words "the", "this", "that", "then"...).
